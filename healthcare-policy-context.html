Core Learning Thesis

Health system government affairs is not only about lobbying. It is about protecting the ability of a large, mission-driven, highly regulated organization to deliver care while managing revenue pressure, labor pressure, payer friction, public expectations, capital needs, and constant policy change.

Plain-language explanation: A hospital system is an essential-service operating company. It must keep serving people whether the economics are easy or difficult. The difference from a utility is that hospitals do not have one clean rate case or one primary regulator that determines cost recovery. Hospitals are paid through a fragmented mix of Medicare, Medicaid, commercial insurance, patient payments, supplemental funding, grants, and other mechanisms.

The value of government affairs is to translate policy into business terms: revenue, cost, workforce, access, payer behavior, capital planning, community trust, and executive risk.

Current Health System Pressures

Pressure Data / evidence Business impact Government affairs implication
Labor and workforce AHA reports labor accounts for 56% of hospital expenses. Staffing is the operating model. Labor cost affects beds, throughput, access, quality, and retention. Workforce legislation, workplace violence protections, nurse staffing rules, licensure, graduate medical education, immigration policy, and training pipelines become core advocacy issues.
Medicare / Medicaid underpayment AHA reports hospitals absorbed $130 billion in Medicare and Medicaid underpayments in 2023. Public payer shortfalls pressure margins and may require cross-subsidy from commercial reimbursement. Medicaid cuts, Medicare rates, state-directed payments, provider taxes, supplemental payments, and site-neutral policy become enterprise financial issues.
Medicaid funding risk IHA reports Illinois hospitals face up to $57 billion in federal Medicaid funding cuts over 10 years because of H.R. 1. Medicaid cuts can increase uncompensated care and weaken access for vulnerable patients. Government affairs must connect Medicaid policy to emergency care, behavioral health, rural access, maternity care, jobs, and community health.
Payer friction AHA identifies delayed prior authorization, inappropriate denials, and narrow networks as growing barriers. Creates administrative burden, delayed care, lower cash flow, physician frustration, and revenue-cycle pressure. Prior authorization reform, prompt payment, insurer accountability, denial transparency, and network adequacy become major policy areas.
Supplies and drugs Advocate reported $8.70 billion in supplies and drug expense in 2025. Drug pricing, supply chain volatility, tariffs, and medical-device costs affect operating expenses. 340B, pharmacy policy, PBM regulation, drug-pricing transparency, and supply-chain resilience matter to hospital economics.
Capital reinvestment Advocate reported $2.45 billion in capital expenditures in 2025. Facilities, digital systems, equipment, service-line modernization, cybersecurity, and ambulatory expansion require capital. Payment adequacy and regulatory flexibility affect whether hospitals can invest in future care models.

Utility Industry vs. Health System: Similarities

Similarity Utility industry Hospital / health system Why it matters for government affairs
Essential service Electric and gas service are basic infrastructure needs. Hospital care, emergency care, and specialty care are basic health and community needs. Both industries must explain reliability, access, affordability, and public value.
High regulation Utilities are regulated by commissions, legislatures, environmental agencies, FERC, RTOs, and reliability rules. Hospitals are regulated by CMS, state health agencies, Medicaid agencies, insurance regulators, licensing boards, accreditation bodies, and legislatures. Both require constant translation between technical operations and public policy.
Capital intensity EEI says member companies will invest nearly $1.4 trillion over the next five years, including a record $239 billion this year. Hospitals invest heavily in facilities, medical technology, digital systems, cybersecurity, ambulatory sites, and specialized service lines. Policy decisions shape whether long-term investment is financially sustainable.
Public scrutiny Rate increases, outages, safety, affordability, clean energy, and infrastructure decisions attract political scrutiny. Hospital prices, staffing, access, medical debt, charity care, service closures, and nonprofit status attract political scrutiny. Government affairs must manage reputation, stakeholder expectations, and legislative risk.
Complex stakeholders Customers, regulators, legislators, large-load customers, labor, environmental groups, consumer advocates, and municipalities. Patients, payers, employers, physicians, nurses, unions, regulators, legislators, community groups, universities, and local governments. Both require coalition-building and disciplined stakeholder mapping.

Utility Industry vs. Health System: Key Differences

Difference Utility industry Hospital / health system Plain-language meaning
Revenue model Regulated utilities generally recover approved costs through rates, subject to commission approval. Hospitals are paid through Medicare, Medicaid, commercial insurance, patient payments, 340B, grants, and supplemental programs. Utilities have a more structured recovery model. Hospitals have a fragmented payer model.
Payment certainty Rates and tariffs are approved before billing. Hospitals often provide care first and then face payer review, denial, delay, or underpayment. Hospitals face more uncertainty after the service is delivered.
Service variability A kilowatt-hour or therm is relatively standardized. Patient care varies by diagnosis, acuity, insurance status, complications, social needs, and clinical complexity. Health care is operationally less predictable than utility service.
Regulatory center of gravity Utilities often have a central state utility commission plus federal and environmental overlays. Hospitals deal with many regulators and payers at the same time. There is no single health care equivalent of a rate case that solves the revenue model.
Affordability politics Affordability debates focus on customer bills, rates, energy burden, and cost allocation. Affordability debates focus on premiums, deductibles, out-of-pocket costs, medical debt, hospital prices, and insurer behavior. Both face affordability pressure, but health care affordability is spread across many payment channels.
Public emotion Reliability and affordability are deeply important, but the service is usually standardized. Hospital care often occurs during moments of fear, pain, vulnerability, and family crisis. Health policy becomes personal and emotional very quickly.

Federal Issue Watchlist

Federal issue Current concern Why it matters to Advocate / hospital systems
H.R. 1 / Medicaid reductions and coverage loss Hospital associations warn that Medicaid reductions and coverage losses will reduce access and strain hospital resources. More uninsured or underinsured patients can mean more uncompensated care, emergency department pressure, and weaker hospital finances.
Medicaid state-directed payments and provider taxes Federal policy changes can affect how states finance Medicaid and how supplemental hospital payments flow. Medicaid financing is not just insurance policy. It is a core hospital revenue and access issue.
Site-neutral payment policy Hospital advocates argue that site-neutral cuts ignore the standby capacity, complexity, and regulatory requirements of hospital outpatient departments. Could reduce outpatient reimbursement and weaken the economics of hospital-based ambulatory care.
340B drug pricing Hospitals oppose policies that move 340B from upfront discounts to backend rebates or restrict contract pharmacy access. 340B is tied to medication access, safety-net support, pharmacy economics, and community programs.
Prior authorization / Medicare Advantage The Improving Seniors’ Timely Access to Care Act, H.R. 3514 / S. 1816, would modernize and standardize Medicare Advantage prior authorization. Prior authorization affects care delays, administrative burden, physician frustration, revenue cycle, and patient access.
Price transparency and nonprofit reporting Congress and CMS continue to focus on price transparency, affordability, and hospital reporting. Transparency policy affects compliance burden, payer negotiations, public narrative, and nonprofit legitimacy.
Rural health funding Rural Health Transformation Program funding and rural hospital support remain major federal issues. Rural access is especially relevant to Atrium markets in the Carolinas, Georgia, and Alabama.

How Policy Hits the Business

Policy issue Revenue impact Cost impact Access impact Reputation / political impact
Medicaid cuts Lower reimbursement; more uncompensated care. More pressure on emergency and safety-net services. Could reduce access for vulnerable patients. High political sensitivity because Medicaid affects low-income populations, children, seniors, and people with disabilities.
Prior authorization Delays payment; increases denials and appeals. Adds administrative cost and clinician burden. Can delay care and discharge planning. Hospitals can frame this as patient access and insurer accountability.
340B restrictions Reduces pharmacy-related support for safety-net services. May limit ability to subsidize community programs. Could affect medication access for vulnerable patients. Politically contested between hospitals, drug manufacturers, employers, and policymakers.
Workforce mandates No direct revenue increase unless funded. Can materially increase labor expense. May improve staffing if well designed, but may strain operations if unfunded. Strong public concern around nurse staffing, workplace safety, and care quality.
Site-neutral payment Reduces hospital outpatient reimbursement. Does not necessarily reduce standby, compliance, or facility costs. Could pressure outpatient services, especially in underserved areas. Hospitals must explain why hospital outpatient departments are structurally different.
Medical debt / charity care Could reduce collections. Increases financial assistance obligations. Protects vulnerable patients from financial harm. Central to nonprofit legitimacy and public trust.
Certificate of Need reform Can change market share and growth strategy. May increase capital competition. Could expand access or destabilize existing providers depending on market. Politically framed as competition and access versus system stability and planning.
AI in coverage decisions Could affect denial patterns and appeal volumes. May increase administrative burden if poorly governed. Could delay or restrict care if automated decisions are used aggressively. High public sensitivity because patients distrust opaque denial processes.
Cybersecurity and privacy Ransomware and breaches can interrupt billing and operations. Requires major investment in IT, compliance, and response capability. Can disrupt care delivery and patient safety. High reputational risk because trust is central to health care.

Interview-Ready Positioning

Likely interview question Strong answer angle
Why health care from utilities? Both are essential-service, regulated, politically visible sectors. I understand how to translate operational complexity into policy strategy.
What is different about health care? The payment model is more fragmented. Hospitals provide care first and then navigate Medicare, Medicaid, commercial payers, denials, prior authorization, and uncompensated care.
What would you need to learn quickly? Reimbursement mechanics, Medicaid financing, Medicare Advantage, 340B, payer behavior, hospital finance, and state-specific hospital association priorities.
What value do you bring immediately? Government affairs operations, issue tracking, executive briefing, matrix coordination, coalition management, AI-enabled synthesis, and disciplined multi-state legislative execution.
What is the role really about? Building the operating system behind the government affairs function so the team can execute consistently across states, issues, budgets, briefings, and leadership needs.
How do you think about policy impact? I would ask: how does this issue affect revenue, cost, access, workforce, payer behavior, capital planning, community trust, and executive risk?
Positioning statement: My utility background transfers because I understand essential-service regulation, public trust, stakeholder alignment, legislative strategy, infrastructure pressure, affordability politics, and the operational consequences of policy decisions. The learning curve is not how to work in a regulated sector. The learning curve is the health care payment model: Medicare, Medicaid, commercial payers, uncompensated care, prior authorization, claim denials, and how reimbursement shapes hospital strategy.
